Harmonic Oscillator - Gross Selection Rule Video Transcript

We know that for a harmonic oscillator, the energy levels are equally spaced. We know how to calculate the energy of each of these individual energy levels. We’ve begun to talk about the frequencies of light used to make transitions between these various energy levels, but that leaves the question of which of these transitions is allowed. Am I allowed, for example, to make a transition from the zeroth to the first, from the first to the second, or from the zeroth to the second? Which of these transitions are allowed or acceptable, and which ones are not? When we find the answer to that, we’ll know what are called the selection rules for which of these transitions are allowed.

Remember, the way we determine which transitions are allowed is by using the transition dipole moment for the molecules, which is proportional to this quantity. If I want to make a transition from, let’s say, the \(n\)-th to the \(n'\)-th energy level, from \(E_0\) to \(E_1\), or from \(E_0\) to \(E_2\), I need to know the integral of a wave function for the initial state and a wave function for the final state, sandwiched around the dipole moment of the molecule in the direction parallel to the oscillation of the light that we are shining on the molecule.

For a molecule like HCl, which has a dipole moment that points in a certain direction because it has a positive side and a negative side, we might be interested in that dipole moment, or depending on the direction the light is pointing, the component of the dipole moment that’s in the \(z\)-direction. So, let’s think about what this integral would equal. As a first attempt, we can say that if the dipole moment happens to be constant—if the dipole moment of HCl is just a number, say 2 or 3, whatever the dipole moment of that molecule is—if I pull that constant out of the integral, and as a spoiler alert, this is going to be not the correct thing to do. If I’ve raised any eyebrows by pulling that dipole moment out of the integral, you’re correct to be skeptical at this point.

But if I do pull the dipole moment out of the integral as a constant and then ask what this integral is, we know how to do that. Before we even plug in the wave functions and compute the integral, what is the integral of one wave function multiplied by a different wave function, a harmonic oscillator wave function multiplied by a different harmonic oscillator wave function, one of which has been complex conjugated? That is guaranteed to be zero because those wave functions are orthogonal to one another. Every pair of wave functions—0 and 1, 0 and 2, 1 and 2—are mutually orthogonal, so this integral is guaranteed to come out to be equal to zero. Thus, the whole integral must be zero, suggesting that none of these transitions are allowed. We can’t use light to excite vibrational transitions in molecules, and we know that’s not true. We know that infrared light is absorbed by diatomic molecules to excite some of these vibrational transitions; we just want to know which ones are allowed.

So, the error we’ve made here—let me cross this out—is that the \(z\)-component of the dipole actually depends on this integration variable that I haven’t bothered to write down yet. Remember, for the harmonic oscillator, the variable that the wave functions depend on is \(x\), the bond displacement, the amount of stretch in this chemical bond, the covalent bond. When \(x\) is positive, the bond has been stretched; when \(x\) is negative, the bond has been compressed. So, I’m integrating over \(x\), and in fact, the dipole moment of this molecule does depend on \(x\). We may like to think of HCl as just having some constant dipole moment, but in fact, there is some dependence on \(x\). I can say it has some constant dipole moment and then some rate of change, some linear term; it either goes up or down as I stretch the bond. In fact, it will go up. This dipole moment will increase as I stretch the bond and make it longer. The distance between the atoms has increased, and I also have to consider the size difference of the charges on the two ends of the molecule. Thus, the dipole moment will get longer as I stretch the bond, so it depends on the value of the stretch of the bond.

The more correct way to approach this problem would be to say my integral looks like the initial wave function, the dipole moment, the \(z\)-component of the dipole moment, which looks like some \(\mu_0\), some default dipole moment when the bond is not stretched at all, plus some \(\frac{d\mu}{dx}\), how quickly the dipole moment changes as I stretch the bond, multiplied by \(x\), and then the ending state that I’m interested in. So that’s what I actually want to integrate over \(x\). When I do that, let’s go ahead and break this integral up into two pieces, one of which involves the \(\mu_0\) term. So, that’s going to look like \(\psi_n^* \mu_0 \psi_{n'} \, dx\). The second piece will come from the second half of the term in parentheses, so that integral will look like \(\psi_n^* \frac{d\mu}{dx} \cdot x \cdot \psi_{n'} \, dx\).

Now, because of how I described the problem, there’s some constant original dipole moment plus the change that reflects the dependence on \(x\). This term is constant; this is just the default dipole moment when there’s no stretch to the bond, so I can pull it out of the integral. What’s left will then be just \(\psi_n \cdot \psi_{n'}\), and that integral will, in fact, be zero. So, the first term is not important; it doesn’t matter how big the dipole moment is, whether the dipole moment is large or small, this term is going to go away completely. The second term, what I can pull out of the integral, at least approximately in this case, is that I certainly can’t pull \(x\) out of the integral—that’s the variable I’m integrating over—but what I can at least approximately pull out of the integral is the rate of change of the dipole moment as I change \(x\). If I stretch the bond a little bit and the dipole moment goes up a tenth of a Debye per angstrom, if I stretch it a little bit more, it will go up by roughly the same amount. So, if I pull this term out of the integral, I get this result.

Now I have two wave functions sandwiched around \(x\), and I don’t know for sure that this is going to be zero, so that’s going to take a little more thought, and we’ll postpone that until the next video. But remember, what we’re interested in is finding out when this transition dipole moment is equal to zero, which means the molecule cannot absorb light to make that transition, and when this integral is not equal to zero, then the molecule can absorb light to make that transition. That will tell us which of these transitions are allowed or forbidden.

If we look for ways to make this integral zero, this part has already gone away. If I look for ways to make this integral equal to zero, I can either do it by making the term out front equal to zero or by making the integral itself equal to zero. The integral is a little more complicated; that’s what we’ll postpone. But certainly, if that quantity is zero, then both terms are zero, and the transition dipole moment disappears, making the transition forbidden.

What that leads to is our gross selection rule, which I’ll put here: \(\frac{d\mu}{dx}\), the rate of change of a dipole, the amount by which the dipole changes as I change the bond length, must be non-zero in order for a molecule to absorb light and change its vibrational state. If a molecule has \(\frac{d\mu}{dx} = 0\), then both these terms will be zero, the transition dipole moment will be zero, and the transitions are forbidden.

For example, if I have some molecules, like HCl, the one we’ve just been talking about, as we’ve illustrated, if I stretch that bond, the two charges get further apart, and the dipole moment changes. So, the dipole moment is changing as I change the bond length. I don’t need to know the size of this number; I know that the dipole moment changes as I’m changing the bond length, so that molecule can absorb infrared light. We say that that molecule is infrared active; in other words, it’s active in the infrared spectrum. If I shine infrared light on this molecule, there are some frequencies that it will absorb. Likewise, other molecules with dipole moments, such as carbon monoxide, which has a relatively small dipole moment, will also be infrared active. That dipole moment will change as I change the bond length.

On the other hand, if I take non-polar molecules like \(\text{H}_2\), \(\text{N}_2\), \(\text{O}_2\), \(\text{Cl}_2\), and so on, those molecules—here’s an \(\text{H}_2\) molecule—have no dipole moment. The dipole moment is zero, but more importantly, when I stretch the bond, the dipole moment is still zero. The two hydrogens have the same charge, so the dipole moment is not changing when I change the bond length. Because it violates this gross selection rule, those molecules will not absorb infrared light. We say that those molecules are infrared inactive, meaning that they are not active in the infrared spectrum. If I shine infrared light on these molecules, they won’t absorb any of it in order to change their vibrational energy levels.

So, that leaves us with one question: how do we know which molecules can absorb light at all? We haven’t yet made any progress on knowing which pairs of energy levels we can make transitions between. To do that, we’ll have to go back and look at this integral and find out which \(n\) and \(n'\) leave this integral zero or non-zero, and that will tell us the rest of the selection rules for the harmonic oscillator.
