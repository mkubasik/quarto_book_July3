We are mainly interested in using the harmonic oscillator model to treat the vibration of diatomic molecules. Any time we have a potential energy that looks like some variable squared, it ends up being called the harmonic oscillator model. It’s worth spending a few minutes explaining why this model is called a harmonic oscillator and what it looks like when you’re not solving quantum mechanical problems with it.

This expression for potential energy looks like the square of some variable. Let me draw what that potential energy would look like in graphical form. There is some variable, which for us might be a bond length but could generally represent many different things. The potential energy will look like the square of that variable, increasing symmetrically on either side of zero for that variable, resembling a parabola. Any variable that behaves like this, whose potential energy behaves like the square of that variable, will act essentially like a classical Hooke’s Law spring.

For example, consider a spring with an object at the end, maybe attached to a wall. If I stretch that spring and let it go, it will snap back and then oscillate back and forth. If I compress the spring, it will snap forward and then oscillate back and forth. The reason for this behavior on the potential energy curve is that if I move the value of \(x\) to some positive number where the energy is large, it will be able to reduce that positive energy by moving the \(x\) coordinate smaller, and the particle will slide down this potential energy well, sliding back up to a point on the other side where the potential energy is equal to where it started. Likewise, if I compress it, it will roll back and forth.

I can think of it as a spring, or as placing a ball inside of a parabolic bowl that will roll back and forth, or as a diatomic molecule, which is a lot like a spring. The reason we call it a Hooke’s Law spring is that the more I stretch it, the larger the restoring force gets, and the restoring force is proportional to the amount of stretch. Mathematically, what we are saying is that the force, if I stretch this spring, is the negative derivative of potential energy with respect to position. Since I know what \(V(x)\) is, I can write:

[ F = - = -( k x^2) = -kx ]

This is our Hooke’s Law spring. If I double the stretch in the spring, I have doubled the restoring force in the negative direction that the particle feels. The other thing we should note is that this constant \(k\), when we talk about springs, is called the force constant. A strong, stiff spring, one that is very hard to stretch because the energy rises quickly as I stretch it, has a large value of \(k\) or a large force constant. A very soft spring with a low force constant would be one that is very easy to stretch, like a slinky, for example.

We also know about the behavior of a system like this that relates to force. The other physics equation that you may be familiar with is \(F = ma\), which relates mass times acceleration, or if we prefer, mass times the second derivative of position with respect to time. Since the variable we use for mass when discussing the harmonic oscillator is not \(m\) but typically the reduced mass of our object, we will write this as \(\mu \frac{d^2x}{dt^2}\).
