Even though we normally think of light as behaving like a wave, we’ve seen that it’s actually made of particles. Sometimes, it behaves like a particle when we consider individual photons of light. Individual particles of light are required to be thought of as particles instead of waves. It turns out that matter also behaves a little bit schizophrenically like this. Normally, we think of matter—atoms, molecules, baseballs—as being composed of individual particles. However, it is sometimes necessary to think of matter as behaving like a wave. These are the two different aspects of what’s called wave-particle duality. Every quantum mechanical object, whether it’s a photon of light, an atom, a molecule, or an electron, has what’s called wave-particle duality. Sometimes it behaves like a particle, and in other ways, it behaves like a wave.

In particular, when we think of matter having wave-like properties, it begins to sound a little bit weird. So let’s talk more about what that means. The different properties of matter that show it is, in some respects, like a wave include that matter is delocalized. We are used to making statements about matter that describe its particle behavior; it has a position, it has momentum, and we can describe where that particle is and where it’s going. But at other times, like a wave, if I ask you where a wave is in an ocean, the wave is delocalized—it’s spread out over some region of space. The same thing turns out to be true about small quantum mechanical objects as well.

Two other things that particles can do, which matter can also do, are diffraction and interference. If I take two waves and cross them, they can either interfere constructively or destructively, depending on whether two troughs or peaks hit at the same time or whether a peak hits at the same time as a trough, resulting in destructive interference. That type of interference and diffraction—when a wave bends as it passes around an object or through different media—are properties that we think of as describing how waves behave, but they also describe how matter behaves under certain circumstances.

To delve deeper into this for small quantum mechanical particles, let’s talk about something called the de Broglie wavelength. It’s not surprising when we talk about the wavelength of light or the wavelength of a wave; the wavelength being the distance between two troughs or two peaks of a wave. The length of a wave turns out to be applicable to particles as well, in a particular form we call the de Broglie wavelength. I can calculate the wavelength of a quantum mechanical particle as Planck’s constant divided by the momentum of the particle, which is mass times velocity.

So, what does the wavelength of a particle mean? If I ask, “What’s the wavelength of this marker?” or “What’s the wavelength of some baseball or some physical object?” it’s hard to even understand what that means. Let’s do a few calculations to see what these wavelengths are like.

Let’s say a particle is moving with a velocity of 100 meters per second, and we want to know what its wavelength is. I’ve given you the velocity, we know Planck’s constant, and the only other thing we need to know is the mass. We’ll do this a few different times, first for the smallest particle we can think of, at least in a chemistry class, which would be an electron. The mass of the electron is \(9.1 \times 10^{-31}\) kilograms. That’s a tiny mass.

If we do the full calculation, we can calculate the wavelength. The way we do this is by running through it exactly once: the wavelength is Planck’s constant divided by mass times velocity. Planck’s constant, which is in joule-seconds, divided by the mass \(9.1 \times 10^{-31}\) kilograms, divided by the velocity of 100 meters per second.

To make the units work out, we have to recognize that the joule in Planck’s constant is a composite unit; one joule is one kilogram meter squared per second squared. Once we remember that, if I multiply by seconds, that gets rid of one of these seconds. Dividing by kilograms in the denominator gets rid of kilograms, and dividing by meters per second gets rid of one of these meters and the other seconds. All I’m left with is meters.

If I plug those numbers into a calculator, I find that I get \(7 \times 10^{-6}\) meters, or seven micrometers. So, an electron, according to this equation, has a de Broglie wavelength of seven micrometers when it’s flying around at 100 meters per second. Seven micrometers is small; \(7 \times 10^{-6}\) meters, but it’s certainly a lot larger than an electron itself. The electron itself is no bigger than \(10^{-10}\) meters or so, so that is considerably bigger—many orders of magnitude bigger than the electron itself. The size of the electron, the radius of the electron, will be important in just a second after we consider the next few examples.

Let’s consider another small quantum mechanical object, but not quite as small—a single atom. Maybe a hydrogen atom, or let’s take a somewhat larger atom, the mass of a carbon atom, for example. We know that the mass of a carbon atom is 12 grams per mole. If I convert that to kilograms, convert grams to kilograms, and use Avogadro’s number to get rid of grams from the per mole, that turns out to be about \(1.0 \times 10^{-29}\) kilograms or so, maybe \(1.0 \times 10^{-28}\) kilograms. That’s several thousand times heavier than an electron.

When I use that mass in the de Broglie wavelength calculation, it turns out that the de Broglie wavelength of a carbon atom flying around at 100 meters per second works out to be \(3 \times 10^{-10}\) meters. In SI units, that would be about three angstroms, and a hydrogen atom itself is about half an angstrom in size. So, that’s larger than, or at least on the same order of magnitude as, the atom itself—it’s comparable to the size of the atom.

If I continue with an example that’s a little bit larger, let’s combine several atoms together and make a molecule. It’s not really important what molecule we’re talking about; maybe a benzene molecule or a caffeine molecule, or some molecule made up of a handful of atoms. Let’s say, roughly speaking, I’ve combined a bunch of carbons and hydrogens and other atoms together. That molecule might have a mass of somewhere around 100 grams per mole.

If we calculate the de Broglie wavelength of that molecule, we find that, again, because the mass is in the denominator and this mass is getting larger, the de Broglie wavelength is going to get shorter. So, this de Broglie wavelength has gotten a little bit shorter; the mass has gotten bigger by almost a factor of 10, so the de Broglie wavelength got shorter by almost a factor of 10. It’s now only a fraction of an angstrom. The molecule itself is now several angstroms large because I’ve combined several molecules, each of which might be about an angstrom in size.

This size is now smaller than the original object, so the de Broglie wavelength of a molecule is smaller than the molecule itself when the velocity is about 100 meters per second. As a last example, let’s compare those small quantum mechanical objects—atom, electron size—with something large, something that we can see in the real world, like a baseball, a pen, or a marker. Just as a rough estimate, let’s say we’re talking about something with a mass of 1 kilogram (2.2 pounds or so).

If I plug that number into the equation for the de Broglie wavelength, this is now many orders of magnitude heavier than an individual molecule. What I find is that the de Broglie wavelength has gotten quite a bit shorter. If I use a kilogram moving at 100 meters per second, I find that the de Broglie wavelength of that object is \(1 \times 10^{-36}\) meters—ridiculously small. An atom, remember, is only about \(1 \times 10^{-10}\) meters in size, so this is about 26 orders of magnitude smaller than an individual atom.

The wavelength of this macroscopic object is so small that we can’t even measure it, see it, or detect it in any way. It’s not only smaller; it’s much, much smaller than the original object. That last statement is why it seems a little bit nonsensical for us to talk about the wavelength of a marker or the wavelength of a baseball or something like that. The wavelength itself is so small that we can’t measure it effectively.

What this wavelength means is the same thing it would mean for a water wave, a pressure wave, or a sound wave. It’s the length of the wave that determines how that thing diffracts or interferes with itself. It’s sort of nonsensical to say that if I throw two baseballs at each other and they collide, they can interfere constructively or destructively. Of course, that’s not true; they just collide the way particles do. This is because the delocalization or the interference of those baseballs happens on a size that’s only a tiny fraction of the molecules themselves.

Only undetectable levels do they interfere or diffract off of one another, whereas quantum mechanical objects like molecules, atoms, or electrons have wavelengths that are comparable to, or sometimes much larger than, the objects themselves. They can actually behave more like a wave than a particle if their de Broglie wavelengths are much larger than the size of the particles themselves.

This certainly seems weird at the macroscopic level because we are not used to seeing objects behave that way, but at the quantum-mechanical, small atomic level, things definitely do behave. Matter does behave like a wave. Let me illustrate for you one of the ways we know this is true, or one of the first pieces of evidence we had that illustrated that small objects do, in fact, diffract and interfere the way waves do. This is an experiment called the two-slit experiment.

To start with, I’ll describe what a one-slit experiment would look like when we’re talking about not particles but waves. Let’s imagine a wave, like a wave in the ocean. Imagine a line of waves, a top view of a line of waves that are moving in the ocean, about to break on the shore. Before they reach the shore, they get to a wall that someone has built near the shore that has a gap in it.

What’s going to happen? If the wall were solid, the waves would just crash into the wall and wouldn’t move past it. But if we put a gap in the wall, or a slit in the wall, what happens when the waves reach the slit is that they won’t pass through regions where the wall is solid; they will pass through the gap, but they’ll diffract around that opening. The ripples will move out in the same pattern that you see if you threw a rock into a pond—the ripples would move out in a circular pattern.

That’s how we understand waves behave in a two-slit experiment. If our wall has two gaps in it and again we have waves approaching the wall with the slits, what will happen is the waves will diffract out from these openings. The ripples that get formed when the waves diffract out of these openings will begin to overlap themselves at some point. The places where I’ve drawn the troughs or peaks of these waves occurring at the same place will be sites of constructive interference, where the wave will be twice as high as it would be if there were only one wave from a single slit.

There will be various places where there’s constructive interference and various other places where there’s destructive interference, where the peak of one wave overlaps with the trough of another wave. So, we’ll see constructive and destructive interference the way we’d expect normal waves to behave.

Now, things get a little bit weird when I imagine what should happen and what actually does happen for particles. These are waves moving towards a wall with some gaps in it. I can also show what happens when particles arrive at a wall with gaps in it. Let’s imagine we’re throwing baseballs or shooting bullets at a wall with a gap in it.

Instead of a lot of waves, I have a lot of particles that I’m sending toward this gap. Certainly, what we’d expect to happen is that a particle that hits the wall won’t pass through, but a particle that arrives at the gap will pass straight through. For one slit, things aren’t too different than what you’d expect. If I then draw another wall back here and ask where these particles hit on the wall, you’d expect them to hit only in places that allowed them to pass through the slit. For the most part, that’s what you see. Occasionally, you’ll get one hitting a little further away because it gets bent or diffracted as it passes through the slit, but that’s not too weird yet.

Where it gets really strange is in the two-slit experiment. If I have particles being sent toward this wall with two gaps and I ask where I find that the particles land after having passed through these slits, again what you’d expect is that maybe particles can pass through here and can pass through here, so they ought to land here and they ought to land here. But that’s not what we see. We see perhaps some particles landing there, but we might see some particles landing here, maybe even more particles landing here.

So, I’m drawing a little histogram or diagram of where we see these particles, and what we see is a series of peaks and valleys. There are certain places where a lot of particles hit, while other places have relatively few particles hitting, and there are some places where no particles hit at all. The only way to understand how that can happen is if these particles, as they pass through the wall, don’t fly through like bullets. Instead, they diffract through the slit and constructively and destructively interfere with one another. The places where we find a lot of particles on this wall are where there’s a lot of constructive interference, while the places where we never find them are where there’s complete destructive interference.

If the particles were purely particles, macroscopic bullets or baseballs wouldn’t behave that way, but small atoms and electrons do, in fact, behave that way. The only way we can understand that they do is the fact that these electrons and atoms behave as much like waves as they do like particles and can constructively and destructively interfere with each other.

That was one of the first experiments that let us know for sure that small objects do, in fact, behave like waves. This completes our tour of the quantum mechanical properties at the qualitative level that you may already know something about. Our next step will be to get a little more quantitative and to understand quantum mechanics at the level of equations and numbers so that we can begin to make concrete predictions.
