We’ve seen that the one-dimensional particle in a box is a problem that we can solve using Schrödinger’s equation. Here’s a reminder of what Schrödinger’s equation looks like for the one-dimensional particle in a box. We gained some insights from solving that problem and discovered that it’s a useful model for describing things like an electron in a linear conjugated hydrocarbon molecule. It may also be useful for a small number of other scenarios. However, in general, problems in the three-dimensional world are not well described by a one-dimensional particle in a box. Therefore, we will gain much more knowledge by solving the same particle in a box problem in three dimensions rather than just one dimension. That’s our next task.

To recap how we approached the one-dimensional particle in a box, we started by writing down Schrödinger’s equation. There is a kinetic energy term, and the potential energy term was zero because we assumed the particle has no potential energy. So, that’s our one-dimensional particle in a box Schrödinger’s equation. We could then solve Schrödinger’s equation to write down the wave functions. Any function that resembles a sine wave will have a second derivative that is the negative of the original function, so these will all be solutions to this Schrödinger’s equation. However, we require that the solutions be zero at the edges of the box, which means we can only have sine waves that end at the edges of the box.

Solving Schrödinger’s equation gives us one set of solutions that obey the boundary conditions, making those sine waves end at specific places defined by the edges of our box. This imposes additional restrictions, and we found that the one-dimensional particle in a box wave function must look like \(\sin\left(\frac{n \pi x}{a}\right)\), where \(n\) is an integer and \(a\) is the box length. This is a quick recap of what we did for our one-dimensional particle in a box. Remember this step: we required that at the two edges of the box, when we plug in \(x = 0\) or \(x = a\), we must get zero for the value of the wave function. This is how \(k\) became \(\frac{n \pi}{a}\).

Now, we will do the exact same thing for the three-dimensional particle in a box. We can start by writing down Schrödinger’s equation. It is still a particle in a box, and we will assume that when the molecule is inside the box, there is no potential energy, and it is not allowed to be outside the box. Thus, we still have just a kinetic energy operator. The only difference is that instead of having \(\frac{d^2}{dx^2}\), we need the full Laplacian, which means we need the derivatives not just with respect to \(x\) but also with respect to \(y\) and \(z\). So, we take the derivatives of the wave function and sum them together, multiplying by the constants out front to obtain a constant times the wave function.

Now we’ve written the three-dimensional version of Schrödinger’s equation, just like the one-dimensional version, but we have \(y\) and \(z\) as well. I should point out that this wave function itself is now a function of \(x\), \(y\), and \(z\) instead of just a function of \(x\) as it was for the one-dimensional problem. If we were to solve this equation formally, as you would have done in a differential equations class, we would invoke separation of variables. We have some \(x\) derivatives that are separate from the \(y\) and \(z\) derivatives.

Without being overly formal, we can write down what the solution should look like and then confirm that those solutions are indeed solutions to Schrödinger’s equation. Since we know how to solve the problem if we only had the \(x\) derivative, the solutions would look like this. That’s a good starting place. Step number two proceeds very similarly to step number two in one dimension, keeping in mind that we’re now in three dimensions instead of one. Instead of having a one-dimensional region confining our particle to keep it in the box, we have a three-dimensional box. The potential energy is zero inside this box, and the particle is not allowed to be outside the box. The wave function must be zero at the edges of the one-dimensional box, and the same must be true for the edges of the box in \(x\). The box may range from \(0\) to \(a\) in \(x\). We can choose a different length \(b\) for the box in the \(y\) direction and let \(c\) be the height of the box in the \(z\) direction.

This means my new boundary conditions require that whenever the particle is at the left edge of the box (when \(x = 0\)), the wave function must be zero. When \(x\) is at the right edge of the box, the wave function must also be zero. This is akin to saying that anytime the particle is at the right face of the box, the entire right side of the box must have a wave function of zero. The first condition is similar for the left face of the box. There are equivalent boundary conditions for the top and bottom of the box, as well as the front and back. Therefore, I also need the wave function to be zero when \(y = 0\), \(y = b\), \(z = 0\), or \(z = c\).

These are the six conditions for the six different sides of the box—not just left and right, but also front and back, and top and bottom. By the time we apply all these conditions, the result will look very similar to the solution in one dimension.
