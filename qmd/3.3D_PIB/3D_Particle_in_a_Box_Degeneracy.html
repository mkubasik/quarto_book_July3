One important feature to be aware of in the energy levels for a particle in a box, specifically a three-dimensional particle in a box, is the degeneracy of those energy levels. To understand this, let’s pay a little closer attention to the expression we have for the energy levels of an individual particle in a box. If I choose \(n_x\), \(n_y\), and \(n_z\) as some integer values, I can calculate the energy of that particle in that three-dimensional box.

Let’s draw an energy ladder. Here are the energy levels for the states of that particle in a box. As I mentioned, for any integers \(n_x\), \(n_y\), and \(n_z\), I can calculate the energy and place that energy on this ladder. The lowest possible energy, or the ground state for that particle in a box, occurs when I make \(n\) equal to one. So, if I choose \(n_x = 1\), \(n_y = 1\), and \(n_z = 1\), that will be the lowest energy level, denoted as \(E_{1,1,1}\).

This energy is equal to

If I ask myself what the next step up on this energy ladder is, I can take one of these ones and change it to a two. Which one I change depends on the relative sizes of the box dimensions. If one box size is relatively large, then it doesn’t cost much energy to increase that dimension. However, in general, without knowing the specific sizes of the box lengths, somewhere above the \(1,1,1\) energy level, there will be \(E_{1,1,2}\), \(E_{1,2,1}\), and \(E_{2,1,1}\) energy levels. Unless the box sizes happen to be equal, there is no reason that the energy for \(E_{1,1,2}\) should be the same as that for \(E_{1,2,1}\) or \(E_{2,1,1}\) because the box lengths will generally not be the same.

I can continue to add more levels to this tree. I can increase some of the twos to threes or have more than one of them be two at the same time. But before we do that, let’s consider what this energy level diagram looks like when I have a cubic box, where the box lengths in \(x\), \(y\), and \(z\) are all equal to each other. In that case, the energies simplify a bit because \(a^2\), \(b^2\), and \(c^2\) are all the same number. I can factor them out of this expression and just compute the sums of these \(n^2\) values.

If I draw what the energy ladder looks like for that case, again starting from the ground state, the lowest energy can be achieved by using ones for each of those constants. Then, \(1^2 + 1^2 + 1^2 = 3\). So, there is the \(1,1,1\) energy level. The next step up on the ladder can be achieved by making \(n_z = 2\), \(n_y = 2\), or \(n_x = 2\).
