Rotational Constant Video Transcript

The energy levels for a rigid rotor model are given by this expression. They depend on the quantum number \(l\), and this formula can be used to calculate energy levels for the first few of these levels, which get further apart as they increase. Now, let’s take a closer look at this expression and point out a few things.

First of all, this quantity, if you are particularly familiar with your freshman-year physics, might sound familiar: \(\mu r^2\). This quantity can be given a name; it is called the moment of inertia. You don’t necessarily need to remember that term from physics or have seen it before, but remember that the moment of inertia means that for rotational motion, what matters for how difficult something is to rotate is not just its mass but how far away it is from the axis that it’s rotating around. For example, if I want to rotate this pen, it is a lot easier to rotate it on an axis close to my body than it is to rotate it on an axis that is far from my body.

The moment of inertia tells us how to combine both the mass of the object and the distance away from the rotational axis. For this rotating diatomic molecule that we are treating as a rigid rotor, instead of having atoms with masses \(m_1\) and \(m_2\) spinning around some center of mass, we have chosen to represent those as an object of mass \(\mu\) rotating at some distance \(r\), the bond length of the molecule, away from the center of mass. So, the mass of this rotating object is \(\mu\), and the distance away from the center of rotation is \(r\). The product \(\mu r^2\) gives us the moment of inertia.

Regardless of whether that concept from a physics class you may have taken helps you understand this equation a little better, or whether you think of it as just a way of cleaning up the notation, we can write the energy levels for a diatomic rigid rotor in this form instead, where we have combined \(\mu r^2\) into this variable \(I\) as shorthand. Sometimes you’ll see these energy levels written in that way; sometimes you’ll see them in a different formula. This simplification uses the moment of inertia for another simplification.

I’ll remind you that Planck’s constant shows up in a couple of different forms, either as Planck’s constant or as this reduced Planck’s constant \(\hbar\), which is the ordinary Planck’s constant divided by \(2\pi\). Thus, \(\hbar^2\) would be \(\frac{h^2}{4\pi^2}\). This looks a lot like \(\frac{h^2}{8\pi^2}\); we just have an extra factor of 2 in the denominator. If we want to simplify things even more, instead of writing \(\frac{h^2}{8\pi^2}\), we often write \(\hbar^2\), leaving that extra factor of 2 in the denominator. There is still an \(I\), the moment of inertia, in the denominator, and I still have an \(l(l + 1)\) that I haven’t done anything with. That equation has gotten a little simpler still, so sometimes you’ll see the expression written that way. It’s simpler written in terms of \(\hbar\) than in terms of \(h\).

I’ll continue mostly writing it with \(h\), but you’ll often see it in the \(\hbar\) form. A lot of people like this expression because \(\frac{\hbar^2}{2I}\) behaves a little bit like a mass, though it isn’t quite like a mass. It is very similar to the form that energies take in other systems, like we’ve seen in the particle in a box, which have \(\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\).

If we want to continue this process of making this equation as simple as we can by hiding different constants and variables inside of other redefined constants, we can take it all the way to an extreme. I’ll define this quantity \(B_e\), which I’ll call the rotational constant. Let’s go ahead and take all these constants—everything that multiplies the \(l\) and \(l + 1\)—and lump them into one large constant. So let’s let \(\frac{h^2}{8\pi^2 \mu r^2}\) be this thing that we call the rotational constant.

With that definition, whether we call it \(\frac{h^2}{8\pi^2 \mu r^2}\), or whether we use \(\frac{h^2}{8\pi^2 I}\), or we could even call it \(\frac{\hbar^2}{2I}\), those are all different equivalent definitions of this rotational constant. With those, now all of the constants have been lumped into one, and I can say that the energy levels for this rigid rotor molecule are just the rotational constant times \(l(l + 1)\). Rather than constantly having to divide \(h^2\) by \(8\pi^2\) and so on, if we know the value of the rotational constant, that makes the math a little bit simpler.

Let’s see how that works with an example. Let’s take a molecule like carbon monoxide. It is a diatomic molecule with different masses on one side than the other. That molecule can rotate if we treat it with the rigid rotor model that we’ve developed. I can tell you that the bond length of a carbon monoxide molecule is \(1.13\) angstroms, and I would like to know what the rotational constant for that carbon monoxide molecule is.

Notice that I have to ask not just what the rotational constant is in general for all molecules everywhere; that rotational constant depends on the moment of inertia, the reduced mass, and the bond length of the molecule. So every different molecule—a carbon monoxide molecule, a nitrogen molecule—they all have different masses and different bond lengths, so you can have different values of the rotational constant that will be different for each individual species.

To calculate the rotational constant, I need to know not just the bond length, which I’ve given you, but the reduced mass of the molecule, so we have to calculate that first. The reduced mass for carbon monoxide is defined as the mass of atom one times the mass of atom two divided by the sum of the two masses. For us, atom one is a carbon atom, and atom two is an oxygen atom, or vice versa.

If we calculate atomic masses, we find that carbon is approximately \(12\) grams per mole and oxygen is approximately \(16\) grams per mole. Therefore, \(12 + 16 = 28\) grams per mole for the carbon monoxide molecule, which gives us a reduced mass of \(6.86\) grams per mole.

Now, let’s pause for a moment and discuss that value. What does it mean that the reduced mass of a carbon monoxide molecule is \(6.86\) grams per mole? It’s not the same as the mass of a carbon atom; it’s not the same as the mass of an oxygen atom. In fact, it is lower than either one of those. That’s part of the reason we call it the reduced mass: this mass is smaller than the masses of the atoms that I am combining to make this molecule.

Remember what the reduced mass means: this rotating carbon monoxide molecule that spins about its center of mass is equivalent, in a physics kinetic energy and angular momentum sense, to a single point with mass \(6.86\) rotating around the center, with a bond length equal to the bond length of the molecule. So, it has a larger moment arm; it’s at a larger distance away from the center of mass than either the carbon or the oxygen is. To rotate equivalently, it has a lighter mass and a longer rotational arm. Mathematically, the reduced mass works out to this value of \(6.86\) grams per mole.

We need that value not in grams per mole but in kilograms. To convert, grams cancel, leaving us with kilograms. I don’t want moles, so I’ll use Avogadro’s number to get rid of moles. After the conversion, the reduced mass of this molecule works out to be \(1.14 \times 10^{-26}\) kilograms.

We can use that to calculate the rotational constant of the carbon monoxide molecule. Now, I’ll use the formula \(\frac{h^2}{8\pi^2 \mu r^2}\). We know the values of all those quantities now: Planck’s constant, \(8\pi^2\), the reduced mass we’ve just calculated, and the bond length \(1.13\) angstroms. I’ve given it to you in angstroms, but an angstrom is \(10^{-10}\) meters, so I’ll convert that to SI units and don’t forget to square the bond length.

Now we have all the quantities written in SI units. The units should cancel; in fact, we have kilograms times meters squared underneath the seconds squared. This gives us \(1\) kilogram meter squared per second squared, which is a joule. So, I have joules in the denominator that will cancel one of these joules in the numerator, leaving me with one unit of joules left in the numerator. That makes sense because the unit on this quantity that we’re calling the rotational constant should be in units of energy, as if I multiply that by some integers, I will learn the energy of my rotating molecule.

If we ask our calculator what that math works out to be, we find it is \(3.82 \times 10^{-23}\) joules. That number looks very small. What that number tells us now that we have this rotational constant is that if we want to know the energy levels for this molecule, the math has gotten a lot simpler. Remember, the ground state energy level has an energy of zero. The first state is the rotational constant times \(1(1 + 1)\). Now we can say in our new notation that the energy level is just \(2\) times the rotational constant. The next one up is \(6\) times the rotational constant, and then \(12\) times the rotational constant, and so on.

If we want to know any one of these energy levels, we just take this rotational constant and multiply it by \(0\), \(2\), \(6\), \(12\), \(20\), or whichever level we’re interested in. One advantage of knowing the rotational constant is that now we have an easy way to calculate the energy levels themselves. We can also now ask ourselves how big the gaps between the energy levels are. Is \(3.82 \times 10^{-23}\) joules a big number or a small number?

Remember, the way to answer that question is to compare that energy to \(kT\) to use \(kT\) as our benchmark for energies. To understand whether this gap is large or small compared to \(kT\)—in other words, whether these energy levels are easily populated at room temperature or not—we have to compare those energies to \(kT\), and that’s what we’ll tackle next.
