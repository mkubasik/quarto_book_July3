Rigid Rotor Partition Function - Classical Video Transcript

The ladder of energy levels for a rigid rotor looks something like this, with the states getting further apart as we climb the ladder. The energy levels themselves can be expressed either in terms of the rotational constant or, if we prefer, the rotational temperature. The degeneracy also increases as we ascend the ladder, with this expression giving us the degeneracy of the state of the energy level with quantum number \(l\).

It is an interesting question to ask what the partition function for this rigid rotor is. Now that we know the energy levels, we can calculate the partition function and later use that to determine some thermodynamic properties. As always, the partition function is the sum of the Boltzmann factors, \(e^{-\frac{E}{kT}}\), summed over all the possible states of the system. In our case, the states are all the possible energies corresponding to every possible pair of quantum numbers \(l\) and \(m\). Thus, I need to sum over not only all values of \(l\) (which can go from zero to infinity) but also all values of \(m\) (which can range from \(-l\) to \(+l\)).

This means we have to sum up all those Boltzmann factors. We can simplify that in one step right away because at each level, I have multiple states with the same energy. For example, there are five different states with the same energy when \(l = 2\), or seven states with the same energy when \(l = 3\). The degeneracy tells me how many of them there are, so instead of summing over all the different states, I can sum only over the energy levels \(E_l\) and just remember to include the degeneracy of that energy level.

Thus, we can express the sum as the degeneracy \(2l + 1\) multiplied by the energy \(E_l\), which looks like \(k \theta l (l + 1)\). When I divide it by \(kT\), the \(k\)’s cancel, and I am left with \(\theta_{\text{rotational}}\) in the numerator and temperature \(T\) in the denominator, along with \(l\) and \(l + 1\). So that’s the sum I want to perform over, unfortunately, an infinite number of \(l\)’s: 0, 1, 2, 3, and so on as we climb this ladder.

How do we go about doing that? There are a couple of ways we can consider tackling that infinite sum. First, it may be true that the rotational temperature is quite small compared to the temperature, which means that the energy levels are spaced closely compared to \(kT\). This implies that many of these states are occupied, allowing us to consider a large number of accessible levels without worrying too much about quantum mechanics, similar to classical mechanics where any energy is acceptable and there will be an energy level available. This would be the classical approximation.

On the other hand, if the rotational temperature is not much smaller than the temperature, we will have a quantum mechanical system and will need to consider only the bottom few levels rather than many levels. The good news is that the classical approximation, where \(\theta_{\text{rotational}}\) is much less than the temperature, simplifies this problem. As we have seen for the particle in a box, when we take the classical limit, we can approximate this sum over states (0, 1, 2, 3, and so on) with an integral over all possible values of \(l\).

For case number one, if we are in the classical limit, I can say the partition function is not this sum from zero to infinity, but it will be close to the integral from zero to infinity of the same quantity \((2l + 1) e^{-\frac{\theta}{T} l (l + 1)}\), integrating over all possible values of \(l\). We can indeed compute that integral relatively simply. It may not look super simple, but it yields quite easily to \(u\)-substitution.

The inconvenient part is that \(l^2\) in the denominator, \(l(l + 1)\), is the same as \(l^2 + l\). So, let \(u = l(l + 1)\) to see if we can simplify it. This means \(u = l^2 + l\), and when I make that definition, \(du = (2l + 1) dl\). Notice that \(2l + 1\) is exactly the factor we have out in front of the exponential in this integral. This means the universe is looking out for us and wants our calculus to be relatively effortless today.

Thus, our partition function, with that definition \((2l + 1) dl\), has become \(du e^{-\frac{\theta}{T} u}\). The limits on this integral remain unchanged; \(l\) used to go from zero to infinity, and \(u\) is just \(l(l + 1)\). So, when \(l = 0\), \(u = 0\), and when \(l\) approaches infinity, \(u\) approaches infinity as well.

When I perform this integral, the integral of \(e^{u}\) with some constants out front is \(\frac{1}{\text{constants}} e^{u}\), evaluated between 0 and infinity. I have \(-\frac{T}{\theta_{\text{rotational}}}\), and when I plug \(u\) in first at the upper limit, I have \(e^{-\infty}\) and then subtract the lower limit \(e^{0} - 0\). This is the same as saying \(e^{-\infty} = 0\) and \(e^{0} = 1\), so the quantity in parentheses is negative one. That cancels the negative one out front, and I can conclude that the partition function is \(\frac{T}{\theta_{\text{rotational}}}\).

This is, in particular, the partition function for the rigid rotor of a rotating molecule that we’ve treated as a rigid rotor. I will go ahead and box that result, but let’s make it a dashed box for now because I have some caveats to make about that statement.

First, let’s calculate the value of the partition function for a particular molecule. For instance, if we want to determine the rotational constant at 298 Kelvin for our favorite molecule, carbon monoxide, we find that the rotational temperature of that molecule is 2.77 Kelvin. This is a fairly simple calculation to determine the rotational constant: \(\frac{298 \, \text{K}}{2.77 \, \text{K}}\). Notice that the units cancel, and our answer will be unitless, which makes sense because a partition function is just a sum of unitless exponentials or a way of counting how many states are occupied.

When we divide 298 by 2.77, we get 108. So, what does that mean? The partition function for carbon monoxide, treating its rotational motion, is 108. This means that effectively there are about 108 states accessible to the molecule. Remember that when the temperature is large compared to the rotational temperature, many of the states are occupied, not just a few but roughly 108 states are occupied at 298 Kelvin. Thus, quite a few states are occupied, and our classical approximation was at least somewhat justified.

Now, back to the reason I put this equation in a dashed box: a few caveats about that molecule. First, this equation is only true under the conditions where we’ve derived it. We assumed that the temperature was large compared to the rotational temperature, so we can only use this approximation in the classical limit where the temperature is large compared to \(\theta_{\text{rotational}}\). In this case, the partition function comes out to be a relatively large number.

Additionally, it turns out this equation is only true for heteronuclear diatomic molecules, where the two atoms are different from one another, like carbon monoxide (C and O). If I were to calculate the rotational partition function for \(O_2\) or \(N_2\) or any homonuclear diatomic molecule where the two atoms are the same, I would find that the partition function is not \(\frac{T}{\theta_{\text{rotational}}}\), but rather \(\frac{T}{2\theta_{\text{rotational}}}\).

For heteronuclear diatomic molecules, it is \(\frac{T}{1 \cdot \theta_{\text{rotational}}}\). In general, the more correct formula would be \(\frac{T}{\text{symmetry number}}\), which is one for heteronuclear diatomics and two for homonuclear diatomics. For now, I’ll just note that this equation is only valid for diatomic molecules with different atoms. If we want to calculate the partition function for homonuclear diatomics, just remember to include that factor of 2 in the denominator as well.
